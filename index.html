The future of Latin-America press freedom changes amidst the 2023 elections

Latin America's struggle for press freedom has persistently existed, given the lack of protection in most countries. With elections taking place in a significant number of countries, the future appears uncertain.

By: Ana Valdez Saravia

Dec. 15, 2023

Each country faces its journalistic challenges, particularly during elections that may alter the working conditions for journalists. While some countries have laws protecting journalists and ensuring press freedom, Latin America, as per the latest report from the Inter-American Press Association, currently experiences the lowest level of press freedom in the global average.

From censorship, persecution, and even the killing of journalists, the landscape of press freedom in Latin America has evolved over the years, particularly during elections, which determine the expected degree of press freedom in the country. In 2023, eight countries simultaneously held elections, and reports from Reporters Without Borders and the Inter-American Press Association (IAPA) highlighted the challenges faced by journalism in Latin America.

Both reports provide a global perspective and compare the region with other countries. While some countries in the Reporters Without Borders fall into the danger zone, the IAPA report shows some differences. However, both reports concur that Cuba, Venezuela, and Nicaragua lack press freedom, while Costa Rica and the Dominican Republic have performed better in preserving press freedom. This is not the case for countries like Argentina, Chile, Colombia, Ecuador, Paraguay, Guatemala, and Mexico, which held elections in the same year and also witnessed the killing of journalists.

In the 2022-23 period, 58 journalists were killed, with 43 deaths in 2022 and 15 in 2023, according to the UNESCO Observatory of Killed Journalists. The deaths in 2022 earned Latin America the reputation of being the deadliest region for journalists, according to a report from the Committee to Protect Journalists.

“Although Latin American countries are nominally at peace,” the CPJ stated, “the region surpassed the high number of journalists killed in the Ukraine war.”

In 2023, the aggression against journalists during elections prompted UNESCO to release a statement on November 2, commemorating the International Day to End Impunity for Crimes against Journalists.

Carlos Hugo Jornet, the president of the IAPA Committee on Freedom of the Press and Information, expressed concern about the state of democracy in the Americas. "Talking about full democracies is increasingly difficult,” Jornet stated. “The impact on journalism is direct, and this is clearly reflected in the new edition of the index."
